About Me

Hello, my name is Yuntae Ju. My research focuses on epidemiology, statistical machine learning, and longitudinal data analysis, with a particular interest in understanding how lifestyle behaviors and physiological factors contribute to the development of chronic diseases such as obesity and metabolic syndrome. I am especially interested in leveraging large-scale observational data to identify meaningful patterns and inform strategies for disease prevention and population health improvement.

My academic background originates from quantitative analyses of human performance and physiological data, which gradually led me to broader questions in population health and chronic disease epidemiology. Through this work, I became increasingly interested in the statistical challenges associated with longitudinal and hierarchical datasets, where observations are repeatedly collected from individuals over time and where substantial heterogeneity often exists across populations.

A central theme of my work is the integration of modern machine learning methods with rigorous epidemiologic and statistical principles. While machine learning approaches can offer strong predictive performance and capture complex relationships within data, their application to real-world health research requires careful consideration of study design, validation, interpretability, and generalizability. For this reason, my research emphasizes robust analytical strategies such as grouped cross-validation, resampling-based validation, and model interpretation techniques.

Methodologically, I am interested in problems involving longitudinal prediction, hierarchical modeling, risk stratification, and interpretable machine learning. Many epidemiologic datasets contain complex dependence structures arising from repeated measurements, individual-level heterogeneity, and temporal variation. Developing models that appropriately account for these features is essential for producing reliable and clinically meaningful evidence.

Much of my applied work utilizes observational datasets related to physical activity, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and other cardiometabolic health outcomes as empirical settings for methodological development. I am particularly interested in identifying heterogeneous behavioral phenotypes, improving risk prediction, and generating evidence that can contribute to the prevention and management of chronic diseases at the population level.

My long-term goal is to develop statistically grounded machine learning frameworks for complex epidemiologic data, particularly in settings where prediction, interpretability, and valid inference must be considered simultaneously. Ultimately, I hope to advance methodological approaches that support evidence-based prevention, chronic disease epidemiology, and precision public health.

Representative Publications

Clustering Rookie Korean Baseball Pitchers and Predicting Potential Using Machine Learning

Ju, Y., Ryoo, H.-J., & Suh, S.-H.
Journal of Coaching Development, 2023
This study examines rookie pitchers in the Korea Baseball Organization using machine learning techniques. K-means clustering was applied to identify pitcher archetypes and evaluate their relationship with early career performance indicators. The results demonstrate how data-driven methods can support performance evaluation and talent identification in professional sports.
